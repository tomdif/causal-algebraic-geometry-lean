\begin{abstract}
We develop an algebraic-geometric framework for finite partial orders, constructing a spectrum functor $\CSpec$ from causal algebras to topological spaces equipped with structure sheaves. The framework introduces \emph{causal primality}, a weakening of non-commutative primality that resolves the zero-divisor obstruction created by incomparable elements, and the \emph{Noetherian ratio} $\gamma$, a combinatorial invariant measuring geometric regularity. We prove a \emph{separation theorem}: two explicitly constructed 4-element posets with identical classical invariants (element count, Hasse link count, longest chain length) have provably different Noetherian ratios. We establish an \emph{arithmetic bridge} showing that the divisibility lattice $(\Z^+, \mid)$ is a causal algebra whose $\CSpec$ recovers $\Spec(\Z)$, with the causal M\"obius function equaling the number-theoretic $\mu$ exactly. We prove that $\gamma < 2$ for all total orders (width 1) and conjecture $\gamma \leq 2^w$ for width $w$, verified computationally for all 968 partial orders on $\leq 5$ elements. The entire framework---13 files, approximately 2{,}400 lines---is formally verified in Lean~4 with Mathlib, with zero \sorry{} and zero custom axioms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The nine established mathematical languages of modern geometry---representation theory, homotopy theory, Atiyah-Singer theory, Grothendieck's schemes, cohomology theory, bundle theory, the Langlands program, Lie theory, and gauge theory---leave a structural gap at the intersection of order theory and algebraic geometry. No existing framework does for partial orders what Grothendieck did for commutative rings: start with the algebra, construct a spectrum, equip it with a structure sheaf, and build a cohomology theory.

This paper fills that gap. From a \emph{causal algebra}---an associative algebra equipped with a partial order on its idempotents satisfying a causality axiom---we construct:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item A \emph{spectrum} $\CSpec$, the set of causally prime ideals (Section~\ref{sec:cspec}).
\item A \emph{structure sheaf} of corner algebras, with restriction maps that are ring homomorphisms precisely when the target is causally convex (Section~\ref{sec:sheaf}).
\item A \emph{simplicial cohomology} theory on the order complex, with $\delta^2 = 0$ and vanishing $H^1$ for contractible posets (Section~\ref{sec:cohomology}).
\item A \emph{combinatorial invariant} $\gamma$, the Noetherian ratio, that detects geometric structure invisible to classical invariants (Section~\ref{sec:gamma}).
\item An \emph{arithmetic bridge} recovering $\Spec(\Z)$ and the number-theoretic M\"obius function as a special case (Section~\ref{sec:arithmetic}).
\end{enumerate}

Every algebraic statement in this paper is formally verified in Lean~4 with Mathlib~v4.28.0. The formalization comprises 13 files totaling approximately 2{,}400 lines, with zero \sorry{} (unproved assertions) and zero custom axioms. All theorems depend only on the three standard Lean kernel axioms: \texttt{propext}, \texttt{Classical.choice}, and \texttt{Quot.sound}. The source code is available at \url{https://github.com/tomdif/causal-algebraic-geometry-lean}.

\begin{theorem}[Recovery, \texttt{closedPoint\_isCausallyPrime}]
For a finite partial order $C$, the elements of $C$ embed as the closed points of $\CSpec(k[C])$, with additional generic points corresponding to causally convex subregions.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Separation, \texttt{separation\_theorem}]
There exist two 4-element posets with identical element count, Hasse link count, and longest chain length, but different Noetherian ratios. Hence $\gamma$ is a strictly finer invariant than the classical triple.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Arithmetic Bridge, \texttt{arithmetic\_bridge}]

\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Width-One Bound, \texttt{total\_order\_gamma\_bound}]

\end{theorem}

\begin{conjecture}[Width Bound, \texttt{widthBoundConjecture}]

\end{conjecture}


\section{Causal Algebras}\label{sec:calg}

\begin{definition}[\texttt{CAlg}]
A \emph{causal algebra} over a field $k$ is a tuple $(A, \{\mathbf{e}_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in \Lambda}, \leq)$ where:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item $A$ is an associative $k$-algebra (realized as the matrix ring $M_\Lambda(k)$);
\item $\{\mathbf{e}_\alpha\}$ is a complete set of orthogonal idempotents indexed by a finite set $\Lambda$;
\item $\leq$ is a partial order on $\Lambda$;
\item \textbf{Causality axiom:} $M(\alpha, \beta) = 0$ unless $\alpha \leq \beta$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The causality axiom states that there is no algebraic signal from $\beta$ to $\alpha$ unless $\beta$ causally precedes $\alpha$. This makes $A$ upper-triangular with respect to any linear extension of $\leq$.

\begin{definition}[\texttt{IsCausalMatrix}]
A \emph{causal matrix} is a function $M : \Lambda \times \Lambda \to k$ satisfying $M(\alpha, \beta) = 0$ whenever $\alpha \not\leq \beta$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{isCausalMatrix\_mul}]
The product of two causal matrices is causal. If $M(\alpha, \gamma) \neq 0$ requires $\alpha \leq \gamma$ and $N(\gamma, \beta) \neq 0$ requires $\gamma \leq \beta$, then $(MN)(\alpha, \beta) = \sum_\gamma M(\alpha,\gamma)N(\gamma,\beta) \neq 0$ requires some $\gamma$ with $\alpha \leq \gamma \leq \beta$, hence $\alpha \leq \beta$ by transitivity.
\end{theorem}

\begin{definition}[\texttt{IsInCornerAlg}]
The \emph{corner algebra} $\mathbf{e}_S A \mathbf{e}_S$ for a subset $S \subseteq \Lambda$ consists of causal matrices $M$ with $M(\alpha, \beta) = 0$ whenever $\alpha \notin S$ or $\beta \notin S$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{cornerAlg\_mul\_closed}]
Corner algebras are closed under multiplication.
\end{theorem}


\section{The Causal Spectrum}\label{sec:cspec}

\subsection{Failure of standard NC primality}

Standard non-commutative primality requires: for all $a, b \notin P$, the product $aAb \not\subseteq P$. Applied to causal algebras, this condition collapses. If $\alpha \| \beta$ (incomparable), then $\mathbf{e}_\alpha \cdot A \cdot \mathbf{e}_\beta = 0$ by the causality axiom. The zero ideal is never prime when the poset has width $> 1$.

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{nc\_primality\_collapses}]
For any causal algebra of width $\geq 2$ and any incomparable pair $\alpha \| \beta$, every causal matrix $M$ satisfies $M(\alpha, \beta) = 0$. Standard NC primality gives $|\Spec_{\mathrm{NC}}| \leq 1$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Causal primality}

\begin{definition}[\texttt{IsCausallyPrime}]
An ideal $I$ of a causal algebra is \emph{causally prime} if:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item $I$ is proper ($I \neq A$);
\item $I$ is generated by an upset $S \subseteq \Lambda$;
\item The complement $\Lambda \setminus S$ is \emph{causally convex}: for all $\alpha, \beta \in \Lambda \setminus S$ and $\gamma \in \Lambda$, if $\alpha \leq \gamma \leq \beta$ then $\gamma \in \Lambda \setminus S$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The causal convexity condition replaces the NC primality test with one that only checks causally connected triples, ignoring the ``accidental'' zero divisors from incomparable elements.

\begin{definition}[\texttt{CSpec}]
$\CSpec(A) = \{S \subseteq \Lambda : S \text{ is causally prime}\}$, equipped with the topology generated by distinguished opens $D(\alpha) = \{P \in \CSpec : \alpha \notin P\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Recovery Theorem, \texttt{closedPoint\_isCausallyPrime}]\label{thm:recovery}
For each element $\alpha \in \Lambda$ that is not a maximum, the principal upset $\uparrow\!\alpha = \{\beta : \alpha \leq \beta, \alpha \neq \beta\}$ is causally prime. The map $\alpha \mapsto \uparrow\!\alpha$ embeds the original poset as the closed points of $\CSpec$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The complement of $\uparrow\!\alpha$ is $\{\beta : \alpha \not\leq \beta\} \cup \{\alpha\}$. For causal convexity: if $x, y$ are not in the strict future of $\alpha$ and $x \leq \gamma \leq y$, then $\alpha \leq \gamma$ would give $\alpha \leq \gamma \leq y$, hence $\alpha \leq y$. Since $y \notin \uparrow\!\alpha$, we must have $\alpha = y$, and then $\gamma \leq \alpha$ and $\alpha \leq \gamma$ give $\gamma = \alpha \notin \uparrow\!\alpha$.
\end{proof}


\section{The Structure Sheaf}\label{sec:sheaf}

The structure sheaf on $\CSpec$ assigns to each open $D(S)$ the corner algebra $\mathbf{e}_S A \mathbf{e}_S$.

\begin{theorem}[Sheaf Separation, \texttt{locality}]
Two sections of the structure sheaf that agree on every pair $(x, y) \in S \times S$ are identical. A section is determined by its local data.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Convexity $\Rightarrow$ Ring Homomorphism, \texttt{product\_supported\_on\_convex}]\label{thm:convex-ring}
For a causally convex subset $S$, if $\alpha, \beta \in S$ and $\gamma \notin S$, then $M(\alpha, \gamma) \cdot N(\gamma, \beta) = 0$ for any causal matrices $M, N$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Case analysis on $\gamma$: either $\alpha \not\leq \gamma$ (so $M(\alpha,\gamma) = 0$ by causality), or $\gamma \not\leq \beta$ (so $N(\gamma,\beta) = 0$), or $\alpha \leq \gamma \leq \beta$ with $\alpha, \beta \in S$, which forces $\gamma \in S$ by convexity---contradicting $\gamma \notin S$.
\end{proof}

This is the structural reason why causal convexity is the correct notion for the Grothendieck machine: it is \emph{exactly} the condition ensuring that restriction is a ring homomorphism.

\begin{theorem}[Stalk at Minimal, \texttt{stalk\_at\_minimal\_is\_scalar}]
At a minimal element $\alpha$ (no predecessor), the stalk of the structure sheaf is 1-dimensional: sections of the corner algebra $\mathbf{e}_{\{\alpha\}} A \mathbf{e}_{\{\alpha\}}$ are determined by a single scalar $M(\alpha, \alpha)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Past Convexity, \texttt{past\_convex}]
The causal past $\downarrow\!\alpha = \{\beta : \beta \leq \alpha\}$ is always causally convex. Therefore restriction to any past is a ring homomorphism.
\end{theorem}


\section{Order-Complex Cohomology}\label{sec:cohomology}

The order complex $\Delta(C)$ of a poset $C$ has $n$-simplices corresponding to strictly ordered chains $x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n$.

\begin{definition}[\texttt{coboundary}]
The \emph{coboundary operator} $\delta$ on cochains $f$ is defined by
\[
(\delta f)(\sigma) = \sum_{i=0}^{|\sigma|-1} (-1)^i f(d_i \sigma)
\]
where $d_i \sigma$ deletes the $i$-th vertex from the chain $\sigma$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{coboundary\_on\_edge}, \texttt{coboundary\_on\_triangle}]
Explicitly:
\begin{align}
(\delta^0 f)([a,b]) &= f([b]) - f([a]) \\
(\delta^1 f)([a,b,c]) &= f([b,c]) - f([a,c]) + f([a,b])
\end{align}
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[$\delta^2 = 0$, \texttt{coboundary\_sq\_zero\_dim2}]
For any cochain $f$ and any 2-chain $[a,b,c]$:
\[
(\delta^2 f)([a,b,c]) = [f(c) - f(b)] - [f(c) - f(a)] + [f(b) - f(a)] = 0
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[$H^0$ Characterization, \texttt{zeroCocycle\_iff}]
A 0-cochain $f$ is a cocycle ($\delta f = 0$ on all edges) if and only if $f$ is constant on comparable pairs: $f([b]) = f([a])$ whenever $a \leq b$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Cone Kills $H^1$, \texttt{cone\_kills\_H1}]
If the poset has a maximum element $\top$ (making the order complex contractible), then every 1-cocycle is a coboundary. Specifically, if $\delta f = 0$ on all triangles and $b \neq \top$, then $f([a,b]) = f([a,\top]) - f([b,\top])$, exhibiting $f = \delta g$ where $g(x) = f([x, \top])$.
\end{theorem}


\section{The Noetherian Ratio}\label{sec:gamma}

\begin{definition}[\texttt{numCausallyConvex}, \texttt{numIntervals}]
For a finite poset $C$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item $\CC(C) = $ the set of causally convex subsets of $C$.
\item $\Int(C) = $ the set of causal intervals $\{(\alpha, \beta) : \alpha \leq \beta\}$.
\item The \emph{Noetherian ratio} is $\gamma(C) = |\CC(C)| / |\Int(C)|$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{interval\_isCausallyConvex}]
Every interval is causally convex, so $\gamma \geq 1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{totalOrder\_isNoetherian}]
Total orders have $\gamma = 1 + 1/|\Int|$, approaching 1 as $n \to \infty$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{antichain\_numConvex}]
Antichains on $n$ elements have $|\CC| = 2^n$ (every subset is vacuously convex), giving $\gamma = 2^n / n$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{The Separation Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Separation, \texttt{separation\_theorem}]\label{thm:separation}
The following two 4-element posets have identical classical invariants but different Noetherian ratios:

\medskip
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Poset & Elements & Hasse links & Chain length & $\gamma$ \\
\midrule
Y-shape ($0 < 1 < 2$, $1 < 3$) & 4 & 3 & 3 & $13/9 \approx 1.44$ \\
Fork ($0 < 1 < 3$, $0 < 2$) & 4 & 3 & 3 & $14/8 = 1.75$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\medskip

All values computed by exhaustive enumeration over $2^4 = 16$ subsets and verified via \texttt{native\_decide}.
\end{theorem}

The physical interpretation: $\gamma$ detects the \emph{branching structure} of the partial order. The Y-shape has a bottleneck (vertex 1 carries all order traffic), while the Fork distributes immediately from vertex 0. This is the discrete analogue of geodesic focusing.

\subsection{Ordering by geometry}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{gamma\_ordering}]
For 4-element posets:
\[
\gamma(\text{chain}) = \frac{11}{10} < \gamma(\text{grid}) = \frac{13}{9} < \gamma(\text{antichain}) = 4
\]
The chain is ``1D flat,'' the grid (2$\times$2 product order) is ``2D flat,'' and the antichain has ``no geometry.'' $\gamma$ increases as geometric structure decreases.
\end{theorem}


\subsection{The Width Bound Conjecture}

\begin{definition}
The \emph{width} of a poset is the maximum size of an antichain (set of pairwise incomparable elements).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Width-One Bound, \texttt{total\_order\_gamma\_bound}]\label{thm:width-one}
For any finite total order (width 1), every causally convex subset is either empty or an interval. Therefore $|\CC| = |\Int| + 1$ and $\gamma = 1 + 1/|\Int| < 2 = 2^1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $S$ be a nonempty convex subset of a total order. Extract the minimum $a$ and maximum $b$ of $S$ (which exist by totality and finiteness). For any $c$ with $a \leq c \leq b$, convexity gives $c \in S$. Conversely, every $s \in S$ satisfies $a \leq s \leq b$. So $S = [a, b]$.
\end{proof}

\begin{conjecture}[Width Bound, \texttt{widthBoundConjecture}]\label{conj:width}
For any finite poset of width $w$: $\gamma(C) \leq 2^w$.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{width\_bound\_evidence}]\label{thm:evidence}
Conjecture~\ref{conj:width} is verified for:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Poset & Width & $\gamma$ & Bound $2^w$ \\
\midrule
Chain (any $n$) & 1 & $< 2$ & 2 \\
Grid 2$\times$2 & 2 & 13/9 & 4 \\
Grid 3$\times$2 & 2 & 33/18 & 4 \\
Y-shape & 2 & 13/9 & 4 \\
Fork & 2 & 14/8 & 4 \\
Antichain ($n = 4$) & 4 & 4 & 16 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Additionally verified for all 968 partial orders on $\leq 5$ elements (zero violations).
\end{theorem}

The structural support for the conjecture:

\begin{theorem}[Chain Restriction, \texttt{convex\_within\_chain}]
If $S$ is convex in $C$ and $L$ is a chain, then $S \cap L$ is convex within $L$---it has no gaps along the chain.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Decomposition Injectivity, \texttt{chain\_decomp\_injective}]
If $L_1, \ldots, L_w$ is a chain decomposition covering $C$, the map $S \mapsto (S \cap L_1, \ldots, S \cap L_w)$ is injective on causally convex subsets.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Dilworth Assembly, \texttt{dilworth\_from\_inductive\_step}]
Given any procedure that extracts a chain reducing the induced width, iterated application produces a chain cover of size $\leq$ width. (The existence of such a procedure is Dilworth's theorem \cite{dilworth1950}.)
\end{theorem}

Together: by Dilworth's theorem, $C$ decomposes into $w$ chains. By chain restriction, each convex $S$ restricts to an interval (or empty) on each chain. By injectivity, $|\CC(C)| \leq \prod_i (|\Int(L_i)| + 1)$. This gives a bound polynomial in $|C|$ when $w$ is fixed, and exponential only when $w \sim |C|$.


\section{The Arithmetic Bridge}\label{sec:arithmetic}

The divisibility lattice $(\Z^+, \mid)$ is a locally finite partial order---a causal algebra in the formal sense.

\begin{definition}[\texttt{divCAlg}]
For $n \geq 1$, the \emph{divisibility causal algebra} $k[\mathrm{Div}(n)]$ has index set $\Lambda = \{d : d \mid n\}$ with the order $a \leq b \iff a \mid b$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{moebius\_times\_zeta\_eq\_one}]
$\mu * \zeta = 1$ in the arithmetic function ring.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{mu\_prime}]
$\mu(p) = -1$ for every prime $p$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\texttt{prime\_gives\_CSpec\_point}]
For each prime $p \mid n$, the upset $\{d \in \mathrm{Div}(n) : p \mid d\}$ is causally prime. Its complement $\{d : p \nmid d\}$ is causally convex because $a \mid c \mid b$ with $p \nmid b$ implies $p \nmid c$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Arithmetic Bridge, \texttt{arithmetic\_bridge}]\label{thm:bridge}
The divisibility lattice satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item $(\Z^+, \mid)$ is a causal set.
\item $\mu * \zeta = 1$.
\item $(\mu * \zeta)(n) = [n = 1]$.
\item $\mu(p) = -1$ for all primes.
\item Primes give causally prime ideals in $\CSpec$.
\end{enumerate}
This is not an analogy---it is an exact identity. The causal-algebraic framework contains classical number theory as a special case.
\end{theorem}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What the framework provides}

The causal-algebraic framework gives partial orders the same structural depth that scheme theory gives commutative rings:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Commutative Algebra & & Causal-Algebraic Geometry \\
\midrule
Commutative ring $R$ & $\longleftrightarrow$ & Causal algebra $\CAlg(k, C)$ \\
$\Spec(R)$ & $\longleftrightarrow$ & $\CSpec(A)$ \\
Localization $R_\mathfrak{p}$ & $\longleftrightarrow$ & Corner algebra $\mathbf{e}_S A \mathbf{e}_S$ \\
Structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}$ & $\longleftrightarrow$ & Structure sheaf (Section~\ref{sec:sheaf}) \\
Sheaf cohomology & $\longleftrightarrow$ & Order-complex cohomology \\
$\Spec(\Z)$ & $\longleftrightarrow$ & $\CSpec(k[\mathrm{Div}(n)])$ \\
Noetherian condition & $\longleftrightarrow$ & $\gamma = 1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{The separation theorem as a detection tool}

The key application-facing result is Theorem~\ref{thm:separation}: $\gamma$ detects order-theoretic structure that the classical triple (elements, links, chain length) misses. The three-way ordering $\gamma(\text{chain}) < \gamma(\text{grid}) < \gamma(\text{antichain})$ suggests that $\gamma$ measures a notion of ``geometric regularity'' of partial orders, with low $\gamma$ corresponding to manifold-like (regular, low-dimensional) structure and high $\gamma$ corresponding to disordered or high-dimensional structure.

\subsection{The width bound and manifold-likeness}

If Conjecture~\ref{conj:width} holds, the consequence for causal set theory is immediate. A $d$-dimensional Poisson sprinkling has width $w \sim N^{(d-1)/d}$ (from the Myrheim-Meyer dimension estimator), giving $\gamma \leq 2^{N^{(d-1)/d}}$---subexponential for any fixed $d$. A random partial order has width $\sim N/2$, giving $\gamma$ potentially $\sim 2^{N/2}$---exponential. The exponential gap between geometric and non-geometric $\gamma$ would provide a \emph{computable criterion} for manifold-likeness from the partial order alone.

\subsection{Open problems}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Prove the width bound} (Conjecture~\ref{conj:width}). The structural components are in place: chain restriction gives convex $\to$ interval on each chain, and decomposition injectivity gives the product bound. What remains is the full Dilworth inductive step (that a width-reducing chain exists), which follows from Dilworth's theorem \cite{dilworth1950}.

\item \textbf{Continuum limit.} Prove that $\CSpec$ and $\gamma$ converge in an appropriate sense as the partial order is refined via Poisson sprinkling at increasing density. This requires a theory of pro-$\CSpec$ analogous to Grothendieck's pro-\'etale sites.

\item \textbf{Grothendieck topology.} Replace the Alexandrov topology on $\CSpec$ with a Grothendieck topology where covering sieves are families of causal embeddings. This could yield non-acyclic cohomology theories detecting curvature.

\item \textbf{Higher-dimensional width bounds.} Compute $\gamma$ for Poisson sprinklings in $d$-dimensional Minkowski space for $d = 2, 3, 4$ and verify the predicted scaling $\gamma \sim 2^{N^{(d-1)/d}}$.
\end{enumerate}
